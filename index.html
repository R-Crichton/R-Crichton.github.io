London & the Architectural History

London is a unique city and is the capital of England. The city has something for everyone. The city's history stretches back to Roman times. It is home to the iconic building 'Big Ben'.

After the 'Great Fire of London' in 1666, almost all of London burnt down.

The new buildings after this tragedy followed the baroque design. One of the most iconic architects of this era was Chirstoper Wren. When London expanded, the Georgian style shaped the capital. The focus was on building London's great estates - the Grosvenor Estate and the Bedford Estate. You will mainly find London's Georgian buildings in central parts of the capital.

For a brief period between 1811-1820, the Regency stule left a significant mark on London.

Regency is a stringent form of Classicism, and is directly referencing Graeco-Roman architecture and structures. Georgian features such as sash windows were popular.

The years between 1837 - 1901 marks the Victorian Style. Referencing to Queen Victorias reign. Buildings such as St. Pancras Station and the Houses of Parliament are examples of buildings in a gothic style from this time. In the Victoria period, we were also introduced to mass housing. Most of the homes were built for the middle class.

After the Victorian era, we were introduced to the Edwardian architecture 1901-1910. An era of revivalism by the Baroque and Georgian. The context of this era was a rapid increase of population and new railways. This gave rise to the 'suburbs'. Edwardian homes were often built on larger plots in the suburbs. Edwardian homes can be identified by Dutch gables, deep bay windows & sash windows. The majority of Edwardian homes can be found today in Hampstead Heath, Dulwich, Blackheath, and Richmond.

In the 50's, post-war, we are introduced to the Brutalist architecture. Hundreds of thousands citizens had been evacuated. There was a national emergency and need for housing and reconstruction of a habitable urban envirnment, hence the extended use of concrete. Brutalism can be identified by simple, block-like forms and concrete construction. Examples of brutalism are the Hayward Gallery and National Theatre in South Bank.

In present day, developers continue constructing tall apartment blocks. Unlike the practical and somewhat unattractive designs of the 50's, the buildings now are more modern and luxurious.
